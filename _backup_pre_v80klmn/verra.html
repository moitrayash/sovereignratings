§ Verra AFOLU explorer

The same relative-rating logic that organises the sovereign-credit panel applies, with little modification, to voluntary carbon-market projects. This page applies M1, M2, and M4 to 1,779 Verra-registered Agriculture Forestry and Other Land Use (AFOLU) projects, scored within peer sets defined by methodology, country, and AFOLU sub-category. The data is pulled from the Verra registry's public API (registry.verra.org) on 2026-05-03; the analytical CSVs are available for download below. The page is intentionally tabular rather than chart-heavy: the headline findings are about which projects fit and which don't, and tables let the underlying numbers speak.1

Three findings frame what follows. First, the AFOLU project distribution is heavy-tailed: the top 50 of 263 registered projects account for 80.8 percent of total promised annual emission reductions, and the top 10 account for 42.9 percent. Second, the M-based relative-rating methodology is sensitive to peer-set choice in a way that turns out to be empirically large: the same project routinely receives M4 = 1.00 in a tight peer set and M4 = 0.00 in a loose one, with a mean range of 0.27 across 1,779 projects and a max range of 0.998. Third, a shadow OLS model predicting log(annual ER) from country, methodology, registration year, and sub-category dummies leaves residuals that match the public controversies around specific over-credited and under-credited projects: the Cameroon Boumba-et-Ngoko forest-conservation project promises 459 times the model-fitted baseline, the largest positive residual in the panel.2

1 The dataset

Verra publishes an open API that returns project-level metadata for the Verified Carbon Standard (VCS) program. The query {"program":"VCS","resourceClass":"project"} returns 4,960 projects across all sectors. Of these, 1,779 are AFOLU (Agriculture Forestry and Other Land Use) projects, and 263 are AFOLU projects with a status of Registered. The fields returned are: resourceIdentifier, resourceName, proponent, protocolCategories (top-level sectoral category), protocolSubCategories (these are the AFOLU sub-categories: Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation (REDD), Afforestation, Reforestation, and Revegetation (ARR), Agricultural Land Management (ALM), Improved Forest Management (IFM), Avoided Conversion of Grasslands and Shrublands (ACoGS), and Wetland Restoration and Conservation (WRC)), protocols (the specific methodology, e.g. VM0042 or AR-ACM0003), resourceStatus, country, region, estAnnualEmissionReductions (the figure of interest, in tonnes CO2e per year), version, projectRegistrationDate, and crediting-period start and end dates.

The headline figures from the snapshot are: 4,960 VCS projects across all sectors; 1,779 of those are AFOLU and form the analytical sample; 263 of the 1,779 carry a status of Registered; the registered projects collectively promise 90.0 Mt CO2e of annual emission reductions, of which 80.8 percent is concentrated in the top 50 projects and 42.9 percent in the top 10.

The relevant peer-set-construction question on the AFOLU side is the same as on the sovereign side: against what comparison group does it make sense to ask whether a given project's ER is “high” or “low”? Three peer-set definitions are reported here:

  1. Tight: methodology + country + AFOLU sub-category. A Vietnam REDD project under VM0007 is compared to other Vietnam REDD projects under VM0007. Cohorts are small (median size 3) and noisy.
  2. Medium: methodology only. The same VM0007 Vietnam project is compared to all VM0007 projects globally, regardless of country or sub-category.
  3. Loose: AFOLU sub-category only. The same project is compared to all REDD projects globally, regardless of methodology or country.

3 Peer-set sensitivity (the rater-disagreement finding)

The empirical question: how stable is M4 (the within-peer percentile) when we change peer-set definitions while holding the underlying composite (here, the project's annual ER) constant? The answer is: not very. Computed across all 1,779 AFOLU projects under each of the three peer-set definitions above, the distribution of M4_range (the maximum minus the minimum across the three M4 values for a given project) is:

Across the 1,779 projects, the mean M4_range is 0.27, the median is 0.19, and the maximum observed is 0.998. A non-trivial share of projects therefore receive M4 = 1.00 in one peer-set definition and a value close to 0.00 in another, with the underlying composite ER unchanged.

The intuition behind the spread: M4 is a percentile rank, so it depends entirely on which peers you compare against. Small ARR projects under AR-ACM0003 in countries with only one such project look great in a tight peer set (they are the only project of their kind in the country, so their percentile rank is by definition 1.0) and terrible in a loose peer set (where they are competing on absolute ER against megaton REDD projects in Brazil and Peru). The methodological implication for any rater — commercial or academic — is that M-based scores should be reported as a triple, not as a single number.

This is not a critique of the M-based methodology; it is a robustness check that the methodology survives. The same problem afflicts the major commercial rater systems in the voluntary carbon market today: Sylvera, BeZero Carbon, and Calyx Global all rate carbon-credit projects on letter or number scales that depend implicitly on a peer-set choice the rater does not always document. Public-record disagreements between Sylvera and BeZero on individual REDD projects have been driven by exactly this kind of peer-set choice difference, and the controversy is unlikely to be resolved without an industry-wide standard for cohort definition.4

4 Shadow OLS residuals: who promises more than the model would predict

A complementary cross-check: fit log(estAnnualEmissionReductions) as a function of country fixed effects, methodology fixed effects, AFOLU sub-category fixed effects, and registration year on a balanced panel of 440 AFOLU projects (after dropping singleton categories, the model has R2 = 0.468). The residuals identify projects whose promised ER is much higher or much lower than what the country-methodology-vintage cohort baseline would predict. The residual-positive cluster aligns with the projects that have drawn the most public scrutiny in 2023–2024: the Boomitra grassland-restoration projects, the AR-ACM0003 large-scale agroforestry deployments in India, and the Mai Ndombe-style REDDs in Latin America. The residual-negative cluster is mostly small projects in countries that have produced multi-megaton REDDs — the model expects them to be larger because their cohort baseline is dragged up by the megaton outliers, and they are penalised on residual for being modestly sized in a context where modestly sized is rare. This is itself a peer-set sensitivity result: the same project would have a less negative residual if scored against a sub-category cohort that excluded the megaton outliers.

7 Cross-country: voluntary-carbon-market exposure vs sovereign metrics

Two lenses on the same set of countries. The voluntary carbon market (VCM) places its supply in a particular set of host countries; those same countries also carry sovereign credit ratings, Human Development Index (HDI) scores, and Planetary Pressure Index (PPI100) values that this site computes elsewhere. The natural cross-section question is whether VCM exposure (the total promised ER hosted in a country) tracks any of those sovereign-level measures. The plots below show the country-by-country scatter; correlations on the joint sample are reported in the caption.

Figure 7.1: Country total VCM AFOLU emission reductions (log scale, Mt/yr) vs sovereign composite credit score (latest panel year). Each dot is one country; the largest VCM hosts (Democratic Republic of the Congo, Brazil, China, India, Indonesia) span the full range of sovereign credit. The cross-sectional Pearson correlation is weak (r = 0.12 on linear ER, r = -0.04 on log ER), consistent with VCM project siting being driven by forest-and-land endowments rather than sovereign creditworthiness.
Figure 7.2: Country total VCM AFOLU emission reductions (log scale, Mt/yr) vs HDI. The pattern is similar: VCM exposure does not track development level. The implication is that VCM-hosted decarbonisation flows toward forest-rich and grassland-rich economies regardless of where they sit on the sovereign-credit or development distribution.

8 Project-level ratings: a note on data access

The voluntary carbon market has its own commercial rating ecosystem. The major raters — Sylvera, BeZero Carbon, Calyx Global, Renoster — rate individual projects on letter or number scales analogous to the sovereign-credit agency scales. None of those rater registries are publicly accessible at the project level; they are subscription products. Allied Offsets aggregates several of them, also paywalled. This page therefore uses Verra-only data and our own computed M1, M2, and M4 metrics as a substitute.

The methodological point is that our M-based metrics, computed against country-and-methodology peer sets, function as a proxy for what a public-domain commercial rating system would produce. Where third-party project ratings have entered public reporting (the Sylvera-flagged Mai Ndombe REDD+, the BeZero-flagged Kariba REDD+, several CarbonPlan-flagged AR-ACM0003 grouped projects), the residuals reported in Section 6 above broadly agree with the public-record concerns. The Boumba-et-Ngoko, Cameroon project at residual +6.13 is consistent with the small-IFM-in-Central-Africa over-promising pattern documented in West et al.2 The Punjab agroforestry projects in India at residuals +3.91 and +3.55 fall inside the AR-ACM0003 large-scale agroforestry cluster that has been the subject of public-record critique.

The full residuals are in verra_residuals.csv; the M1, M2, M4 panel against tight peer sets is in verra_relative.csv; the M4 sensitivity panel is in verra_rater_disagreement.csv.

9 Caveats and downloads

Three caveats. First, estAnnualEmissionReductions is the developer-reported figure at registration, not the figure of credits actually issued: this analysis is upstream of issuance and therefore subject to the gap between promise and delivery (which the academic literature on REDD over-crediting has documented at 30–90 percent). Second, the AFOLU restriction excludes the energy-and-industry projects that account for the majority of the broader VCS registry: that is intentional here (AFOLU is the methodologically interesting case for this paper), but a reader interested in comparing forest-based against energy-based credits would need to extend the data pull to protocolCategories = "Energy". Third, registry data does not include the bilateral price at which credits trade: a high-residual project may still produce real reductions, and a low-residual one may be priced into the market at exactly the level its promised ER would suggest. The output of this page is a methodological diagnostic, not an investment recommendation.

The four analytical CSVs are available for download: verra_relative.csv (1,779 AFOLU projects with M1, M2, M4 in tight peer set), verra_rater_disagreement.csv (same 1,779 with M4 across three peer-set definitions and an M4_range column), verra_residuals.csv (440 projects with shadow-OLS residuals, fitted ER, and over-promise ratio), and verra_top50_projects.md (full top-50 table). The complete Verra registry snapshot covering all 4,960 projects across every VCS sector is in verra_projects.csv.
