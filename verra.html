§ Verra AFOLU explorer

The same relative-rating logic that organises the sovereign-credit panel applies, with little modification, to voluntary carbon-market projects. This page applies M1, M2, and M4 to 1,779 Verra-registered Agriculture Forestry and Other Land Use (AFOLU) projects, scored within peer sets defined by methodology, country, and AFOLU sub-category. The data is pulled from the Verra registry's public API (registry.verra.org) on 2026-05-03; the analytical CSVs are available for download below. The page is intentionally tabular rather than chart-heavy: the headline findings are about which projects fit and which don't, and tables let the underlying numbers speak.1

Three findings frame what follows. First, the AFOLU project distribution is heavy-tailed: the top 50 of 263 registered projects account for 80.8 percent of total promised annual emission reductions, and the top 10 account for 42.9 percent. Second, the M-based relative-rating methodology is sensitive to peer-set choice in a way that turns out to be empirically large: the same project routinely receives M4 = 1.00 in a tight peer set and M4 = 0.00 in a loose one, with a mean range of 0.27 across 1,779 projects and a max range of 0.998. Third, a shadow OLS model predicting log(annual ER) from country, methodology, registration year, and sub-category dummies leaves residuals that match the public controversies around specific over-credited and under-credited projects: the Cameroon Boumba-et-Ngoko forest-conservation project promises 459 times the model-fitted baseline, the largest positive residual in the panel.2

1 The dataset

Verra publishes an open API that returns project-level metadata for the Verified Carbon Standard (VCS) program. The query {"program":"VCS","resourceClass":"project"} returns 4,960 projects across all sectors. Of these, 1,779 are AFOLU (Agriculture Forestry and Other Land Use) projects, and 263 are AFOLU projects with a status of Registered. The fields returned are: resourceIdentifier, resourceName, proponent, protocolCategories (top-level sectoral category), protocolSubCategories (REDD, ARR, ALM, IFM, ACoGS, WRC), protocols (the specific methodology, e.g. VM0042 or AR-ACM0003), resourceStatus, country, region, estAnnualEmissionReductions (the figure of interest, in tonnes CO2e per year), version, projectRegistrationDate, and crediting-period start and end dates.

The relevant peer-set-construction question on the AFOLU side is the same as on the sovereign side: against what comparison group does it make sense to ask whether a given project's ER is “high” or “low”? Three peer-set definitions are reported here:

  1. Tight: methodology + country + AFOLU sub-category. A Vietnam REDD project under VM0007 is compared to other Vietnam REDD projects under VM0007. Cohorts are small (median size 3) and noisy.
  2. Medium: methodology only. The same VM0007 Vietnam project is compared to all VM0007 projects globally, regardless of country or sub-category.
  3. Loose: AFOLU sub-category only. The same project is compared to all REDD projects globally, regardless of methodology or country.

3 Peer-set sensitivity (the rater-disagreement finding)

The empirical question: how stable is M4 (the within-peer percentile) when we change peer-set definitions while holding the underlying composite (here, the project's annual ER) constant? The answer is: not very. Computed across all 1,779 AFOLU projects under each of the three peer-set definitions above, the distribution of M4_range (the maximum minus the minimum across the three M4 values for a given project) is:

0.27mean M4_range across peer sets
0.19median M4_range
0.998max M4_range observed
1,779AFOLU projects

The intuition behind the spread: M4 is a percentile rank, so it depends entirely on which peers you compare against. Small ARR projects under AR-ACM0003 in countries with only one such project look great in a tight peer set (they are the only project of their kind in the country, so their percentile rank is by definition 1.0) and terrible in a loose peer set (where they are competing on absolute ER against megaton REDD projects in Brazil and Peru). The methodological implication for any rater — commercial or academic — is that M-based scores should be reported as a triple, not as a single number.

This is not a critique of the M-based methodology; it is a robustness check that the methodology survives. The same problem afflicts the major commercial rater systems in the voluntary carbon market today: Sylvera, BeZero Carbon, and Calyx Global all rate carbon-credit projects on letter or number scales that depend implicitly on a peer-set choice the rater does not always document. Public-record disagreements between Sylvera and BeZero on individual REDD projects have been driven by exactly this kind of peer-set choice difference, and the controversy is unlikely to be resolved without an industry-wide standard for cohort definition.4

4 Shadow OLS residuals: who promises more than the model would predict

A complementary cross-check: fit log(estAnnualEmissionReductions) as a function of country fixed effects, methodology fixed effects, AFOLU sub-category fixed effects, and registration year on a balanced panel of 440 AFOLU projects (after dropping singleton categories, the model has R2 = 0.468). The residuals identify projects whose promised ER is much higher or much lower than what the country-methodology-vintage cohort baseline would predict. The top 10 over-promisers and top 10 under-promisers are below; the full panel is in verra_residuals.csv.

The residual-positive cluster aligns with the projects that have drawn the most public scrutiny in 2023–2024: the Boomitra grassland-restoration projects, the AR-ACM0003 large-scale agroforestry deployments in India, and the Mai Ndombe-style REDDs in Latin America. The residual-negative cluster is mostly small projects in countries that have produced multi-megaton REDDs — the model expects them to be larger because their cohort baseline is dragged up by the megaton outliers, and they are penalised on residual for being modestly sized in a context where modestly sized is rare. This is itself a peer-set sensitivity result: the same project would have a less negative residual if scored against a sub-category cohort that excluded the megaton outliers.

5 Caveats and downloads

Three caveats. First, estAnnualEmissionReductions is the developer-reported figure at registration, not the figure of credits actually issued: this analysis is upstream of issuance and therefore subject to the gap between promise and delivery (which the academic literature on REDD over-crediting has documented at 30–90 percent). Second, the AFOLU restriction excludes the energy-and-industry projects that account for the majority of the broader VCS registry: that is intentional here (AFOLU is the methodologically interesting case for this paper), but a reader interested in comparing forest-based against energy-based credits would need to extend the data pull to protocolCategories = "Energy". Third, registry data does not include the bilateral price at which credits trade: a high-residual project may still produce real reductions, and a low-residual one may be priced into the market at exactly the level its promised ER would suggest. The output of this page is a methodological diagnostic, not an investment recommendation.
