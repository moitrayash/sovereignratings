§ Relative Human Development Index

The Human Development Index, developed by Mahbub ul Haq for the United Nations Development Programme in 1990 and refined by Amartya Sen, is a composite of life expectancy, education, and gross national income per capita.1 It is published annually for roughly 190 countries on a 0–1 scale, with higher values indicating greater “human development.” This page applies the same relative-rating logic as §4 of the Methodology to a re-scaled HDI—HDI multiplied by 100 to align with the credit-rating scale—and asks the natural question: how does a country's standing in the human-development distribution compare to its standing in the credit-rating distribution?

1 Construction

The 2023/24 UNDP HDR composite-indices file2 provides annual HDI values from 1990 through 2022 for 191 entities. Each value is multiplied by 100 to produce HDI100 (range 0–100). For each country–year, the leave-one-out world mean is computed, and four relative measures follow the same definitions as the credit-rating M1–M4:

M1 (HDI)HDI100it2 / LOOit
M2 (HDI)rank of HDI100it within world, year t
M4 (HDI)M2 / N

Inflating to 100 rather than 60 is a deliberate choice: HDI varies over a wider relative range than credit ratings (some sub-Saharan countries are below 0.4, while Norway tops 0.96), so the squared-numerator method is better-behaved on a 0–100 scale than on 0–60. Otherwise the metric is identical.

2 Country explorer

The Compare against control above the charts lets the comparison set be re-cast on the fly. The default is the world (all 142 reporting countries). Same continent restricts the leave-one-out mean and the rank-based M2 / M4 to the focal country's UN-style continent (Africa, Asia, Europe, North America, Oceania, or South America); this makes the most sense for countries whose human-development trajectory is shaped primarily by regional conditions (a Gulf state versus other Gulf states, a Sub-Saharan low-income country against Sub-Saharan peers). Custom subset lets you build an ad-hoc comparison set — useful for SIDS clusters, BRICS, OECD, ASEAN, or any other peer grouping you care about. M1, M2, and M4 are recomputed client-side against whatever set is active.

The first chart shows absolute HDI100 over time alongside the leave-one-out peer-set mean. The second plots the M1, M2 (rank), and M4 (percentile) trajectories. The third overlays HDI100 on the credit composite score for the same country, both on the 0–100 scale (credit scaled up by 5/3) so the two are visually comparable. Most countries' HDI evolves slowly and monotonically; the credit score is more reactive and can fall sharply on a default. Where the two diverge sharply within a short window, the underlying story is almost always a financial crisis whose human-development consequences are absorbed only over several subsequent years—Argentina 2001, Iceland 2009, Greece 2012, Ghana 2022.3

5 Caveats

The HDI is updated annually but is methodologically backward-looking: the latest year's value typically reflects life-expectancy and education data released with a one-to-three year lag. The HDI panel here ends in 2022 because UNDP HDR 2023/24 had not yet released subsequent vintages at the time of this build; updates would extend the series without changing the methodology. The world used for the leave-one-out mean is the union of HDI-reporting countries (191), which is broader than the 150 rated by the three credit-rating agencies. Restricting the HDI benchmark to the rated subset shifts the world mean upward by approximately 4 HDI100 points but leaves country-level relative ranks essentially unchanged.
