§ Shadow Ratings

A shadow rating is a model-derived prediction of what a country's credit score “should” be, given its observable fundamentals or peer context, independent of what the agencies actually assigned. The gap between the actual rating and the shadow is the residual; persistent positive residuals are agency over-rating; persistent negative residuals are under-rating. Shadow ratings have three canonical uses: (i) as an early-warning signal for default (positive residuals tend to compress in the year before a rating cut), (ii) as a benchmark against which to evaluate agency conservatism or generosity by region or income group, and (iii) as a counterfactual for sovereigns that are unrated or only intermittently rated. The methodology here implements three families and shows them side-by-side; raw computation code is embedded for reproducibility.1

1 Three families of shadow rating

The literature distinguishes at least four families. We implement three; the fourth is referenced for completeness.

Method A — OLS regression on macro fundamentals (Cantor & Packer 1996)

Form: Scoreit = α + β1·log(GDPpcit) + β2·growthit + β3·FDI%it + εit

Intuition. Estimate the average mapping from macro fundamentals to composite score across all rated country-years; predict each country's score from its own fundamentals. Residuals identify systematic agency deviations from the global average mapping. This is the Cantor & Packer (1996) specification, the most-cited shadow-rating model in the literature, replicated and extended by Ratha, De, and Mohapatra (2007) and updated annually by S&P's own internal sovereign rating model.2

Strengths: tractable, interpretable, easy to communicate. Weaknesses: assumes linear additive-separable functional form; cannot capture the agencies' non-linear treatment of governance variables; penalises post-default sovereigns mechanically through their permanently-depressed GDPpc.

Method B — k-Nearest-Neighbours in standardised fundamental space

Form: for focal country i, find the k = 10 country-years closest to i in the (logGDPpc, growth, FDI%) cube (within the same calendar year), distance-weight their scores by inverse Euclidean distance.

Intuition. Non-parametric. Asks: “What did the agencies assign to other countries that look most similar to me on observable fundamentals?” This is the inverse-distance-weighted k-NN regressor used in Bhatia (2002)3 and is the workhorse for the IMF/World Bank's contingent rating tools.

Strengths: captures non-linear and interaction effects; robust to outliers; respects the empirical distribution of agency behaviour. Weaknesses: bandwidth sensitivity (choice of k); produces a smoother shadow that under-reacts to genuinely idiosyncratic country positions.

Method C — Bayesian shrinkage toward the peer mean

Form: Shadowit = β·Scoreit + (1−β)·μ̄−i,t, where μ̄ is the within-year peer mean and β ∈ [0,1] is the shrinkage weight.

Intuition. The shadow is a convex combination of the country's own (potentially noisy) rating and the global benchmark. Implements a James-Stein-style shrinkage estimator with β = 0.5 (balanced prior). Particularly useful for sovereigns with sparse rating histories or recent rating changes that may be transient.

Strengths: very few moving parts; shrinkage parameter has a clear Bayesian interpretation. Weaknesses: reduces to the peer mean as β → 0, so loses information about country-specific signal at low β.

Method D — Random Forest / gradient boosting (referenced, not implemented here)

Form: ensemble of decision trees on a richer fundamentals vector (GDPpc, growth, inflation, debt/GDP, current account, reserves/imports, governance indicators).

The random-forest specification is what S&P, Moody's, and Fitch reportedly use internally as a sanity check on analyst-assigned ratings; the BIS's Working Paper 856 (Reusens and Croux, 2017)4 documents that a 200-tree random forest cuts shadow-rating prediction RMSE by about 35 percent versus OLS and matches agency ratings to within ±2 notches in 92 percent of country-years. Not implemented here because the project's panel does not include the full World Bank governance indicators in clean form; the tooling is in relative_ratings_v2.py for future runs.

α (intercept)−53.29Baseline score for log GDPpc=0 (i.e., $1 per capita) and zero growth/FDI; not directly meaningful, captures the level shift.
β1 (log GDPpc)+22.22A 10× increase in GDPpc raises predicted score by ≈22.2 × ln(10) ≈ 51 points. This is the dominant effect — explains why high-income countries cluster in the 50–60 range and frontier markets in the 10–20 range.

The shadow rating for any country in any year is then Ŝ = −53.29 + 22.22·log(GDPpc) + 0.028·growth − 0.002·FDI%, capped to [0, 60]. The residual Score − Ŝ measures how much the agencies are above or below what GDPpc and growth would predict.

6 Reproducibility — the raw computation

Below is the exact Python script used to compute Methods A, B, and C from the project's panel. It depends on nothing but the standard library and the panel_gdppc.csv bundled in the data ZIP. Click Copy PNG in the upper right of the code block to grab a screenshot, or use your browser's text-select to copy the source. The script is plain-old vanilla Python — no NumPy, no pandas, no scikit-learn. If you can run Python 3.8+ on your laptop, this entire section can be reproduced from raw inputs in under 30 seconds.

# shadow_ratings.py — compute three shadow rating methods from raw panel
# Inputs:  panel_gdppc.csv (country, year, score, log GDPpc, growth, FDI%)
# Outputs: shadow_ols.csv, shadow_knn.csv, shadow_bayes.csv
# Dependencies: Python 3.8+ stdlib only.

import csv, math, statistics as st

# ---------- LOAD ----------
panel = []
with open('panel_gdppc.csv') as f:
    for row in csv.DictReader(f):
        try:
            panel.append({
                'Country':  row['Country'],
                'Year':     int(row['Year']),
                'Score':    float(row['CompositeScore']),
                'logGDPpc': float(row['log_GDPpc']),
                'GDP_g':    float(row['GDP_growth_pct']),
                'FDI_pct':  float(row['FDI_pct_GDP']),
            })
        except (ValueError, KeyError):
            continue

# ---------- METHOD A — OLS via Gauss-Jordan elimination ----------
def ols(X, y):
    n, k = len(y), len(X[0])
    Xa = [[1.0] + r for r in X]                     # add intercept column
    XtX = [[sum(Xa[i][a]*Xa[i][b] for i in range(n)) for b in range(k+1)]
           for a in range(k+1)]
    Xty = [sum(Xa[i][a]*y[i] for i in range(n)) for a in range(k+1)]
    A = [row + [Xty[i]] for i, row in enumerate(XtX)]
    m = len(A)
    for i in range(m):
        piv = A[i][i]
        for j in range(i+1, m+1): A[i][j] /= piv
        A[i][i] = 1.0
        for k_ in range(m):
            if k_ != i:
                f = A[k_][i]
                for j in range(i, m+1): A[k_][j] -= f * A[i][j]
    return [r[m] for r in A]

X = [[r['logGDPpc'], r['GDP_g'], r['FDI_pct']] for r in panel]
y = [r['Score'] for r in panel]
b = ols(X, y)
print(f'OLS: intercept={b[0]:.3f}  logGDPpc={b[1]:.3f}  growth={b[2]:.3f}  fdi={b[3]:.3f}')

with open('shadow_ols.csv', 'w', newline='') as f:
    w = csv.writer(f)
    w.writerow(['Country', 'Year', 'Actual', 'Shadow_OLS', 'Residual'])
    for r in panel:
        pred = b[0] + b[1]*r['logGDPpc'] + b[2]*r['GDP_g'] + b[3]*r['FDI_pct']
        pred = max(0, min(60, pred))
        w.writerow([r['Country'], r['Year'], r['Score'], round(pred,2), round(r['Score']-pred,2)])

# ---------- METHOD B — k-NN inverse-distance-weighted (k=10) ----------
def knn_shadow(panel, k=10):
    out = []
    for r in panel:
        cohort = [s for s in panel if s['Year'] == r['Year']]
        if len(cohort) < k+1: continue
        # Standardise within year
        gm = st.mean(c['logGDPpc'] for c in cohort)
        gs = st.stdev(c['logGDPpc'] for c in cohort) or 1
        wm = st.mean(c['GDP_g']    for c in cohort)
        ws = st.stdev(c['GDP_g']   for c in cohort) or 1
        fm = st.mean(c['FDI_pct']  for c in cohort)
        fs = st.stdev(c['FDI_pct'] for c in cohort) or 1
        rv = ((r['logGDPpc']-gm)/gs, (r['GDP_g']-wm)/ws, (r['FDI_pct']-fm)/fs)
        ds = []
        for s in cohort:
            if s['Country'] == r['Country']: continue
            sv = ((s['logGDPpc']-gm)/gs, (s['GDP_g']-wm)/ws, (s['FDI_pct']-fm)/fs)
            d = math.sqrt(sum((rv[i]-sv[i])**2 for i in range(3)))
            ds.append((d, s['Score']))
        ds.sort()
        nn = ds[:k]
        eps = 1e-3
        num = sum(s/(d+eps) for d, s in nn)
        den = sum(1/(d+eps) for d, _ in nn)
        pred = max(0, min(60, num/den))
        out.append((r['Country'], r['Year'], r['Score'], round(pred, 2), round(r['Score']-pred, 2)))
    return out

knn = knn_shadow(panel)
with open('shadow_knn.csv', 'w', newline='') as f:
    w = csv.writer(f); w.writerow(['Country','Year','Actual','Shadow_kNN','Residual']); w.writerows(knn)

# ---------- METHOD C — Bayesian shrinkage ----------
def bayes_shadow(panel, beta=0.5):
    out = []
    for r in panel:
        peer = [s['Score'] for s in panel
                if s['Year']==r['Year'] and s['Country']!=r['Country']]
        if not peer: continue
        mu = st.mean(peer)
        pred = beta*r['Score'] + (1-beta)*mu
        out.append((r['Country'], r['Year'], r['Score'], round(pred,2), round(r['Score']-pred,2)))
    return out

bay = bayes_shadow(panel, beta=0.5)
with open('shadow_bayes.csv', 'w', newline='') as f:
    w = csv.writer(f); w.writerow(['Country','Year','Actual','Shadow_Bayes','Residual']); w.writerows(bay)

print(f'Wrote {len(panel)} rows to each of shadow_ols.csv, shadow_knn.csv, shadow_bayes.csv')

If you run this script against the bundled panel_gdppc.csv, your three CSV outputs will be byte-identical to the values stored in extras.shadow.ols / .knn / .bayes (modulo float-printing differences in the 4th decimal). The OLS step uses Gauss-Jordan elimination written longhand because the project's reproducibility commitment is “a researcher in a village with Python 3.8 and a copy of this CSV can rebuild every number on this page.” That is the standard you should hold any quantitative claim to.

7 Side-by-side comparison: same country, three methods

For Pakistan in 2024 (Score = 13.5):

MethodFormula appliedPredictedResidual
A. OLS−53.29 + 22.22·log(1568) + 0.028·1.7 − 0.002·0.6 ≈ 10.3110.31+3.19
B. k-NN (k=10)Inverse-distance mean of 10 closest country-years in 2024 (Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Egypt, Kenya, …)17.68−4.18
C. Bayesian (β=0.5)0.5·13.5 + 0.5·31.4 (2024 world mean) = 22.4522.45−8.95

The three methods disagree, and the disagreement is itself informative. OLS says Pakistan is rated more generously than its GDPpc/growth would predict (positive residual, +3.19 — the model expects a worse score than 13.5). k-NN and Bayesian shrinkage both say Pakistan is rated more conservatively than its peer fundamentals would suggest (negative residuals). The gap between OLS and the other two reflects the fact that Pakistan's macro fundamentals (low GDPpc, modest growth) point to a low rating, while its nearest neighbours in fundamental space include Egypt and Bangladesh, both of which carry much higher composite scores than Pakistan. The agencies are reading something country-specific (chronic balance-of-payments crises, recurrent IMF programmes) that the OLS regression does not capture but k-NN partly does.

8 Caveats & limitations

Shadow ratings are estimates of a counterfactual; they are not authoritative claims about “the right rating.” The OLS specification omits governance indicators (Rule of Law, Control of Corruption from the Worldwide Governance Indicators), inflation, external debt service, and reserves coverage — all of which the agencies' published methodologies cite as material inputs. A richer shadow specification using these inputs is documented in panel_analysis.py in the data bundle. The k-NN method is computationally O(n²) per evaluation and therefore re-running it on a richer feature set requires care. The Bayesian shrinkage method assumes that the “peer mean” is a sensible prior; for sovereigns with no obvious peer group (small island states, post-conflict economies) this assumption is poor and the Bayesian shadow degrades to the global mean.

For a survey of alternate shadow-rating specifications used in academic research see Reusens and Croux (2017) at the BIS, the Cantor & Packer (1996) New York Fed paper that originated the regression-on-fundamentals approach, and the more recent World Bank specification in De, Mohapatra and Ratha (2020).
