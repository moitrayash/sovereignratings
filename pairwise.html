§ Pairwise & Distance-Graded Ratings

The relative rating methods documented in §4 of the Methodology all use a peer-group benchmark—an unweighted average of N−1 other countries. Two finer-grained variants extend the same idea: a pairwise measure that compares one country directly against another, and a distance-graded measure that weights every other country by inverse-distance from the focal capital.1 Both are continuous functions of the same underlying composite scores, so the qualitative narrative carries over; both isolate finer signal than a flat group average.

1 Pairwise: country-vs-country

For any two countries A and B in any year t, the pairwise relative score is simply the difference of their composite scores:

Pairwise relative score PABt = ScoreAt − ScoreBt

This is the simplest relativization in the De, Mohapatra, and Ratha (2020) family2—rel_eq with a one-country “peer group”. Pairwise series are less volatile than within-group methods because two countries' rating trajectories are usually persistent and correlated; reversals when they occur are therefore informative. The series also has a clean economic reading: a positive value means A's rating sits N points above B's on the 0–60 scale, equivalent to roughly N/3 letter-grade notches.

The two charts above plot the absolute composite scores of the chosen pair (top) and their pairwise spread PABt (bottom). Sustained widening of the spread is most often driven by one side's structural deterioration rather than the other's improvement; sustained narrowing typically reflects convergence after a crisis. When both lines move together, the spread is roughly invariant—the pair is co-rated within a common regional or income cluster.

2 Distance-graded relative ratings

The pairwise measure compares two countries; a flat group average treats all peers equally. A distance-graded measure interpolates between these two extremes by weighting every other country by an inverse-distance kernel:3

Distance-weighted benchmark Bit(d) = (Σj≠i wij Scorejt) / (Σj≠i wij)

Kernel wij = exp(−λ · (dij / 1000)p)

Distance-graded relative score Rit(d) = α · Scoreit − Bit(d)

where dij is the great-circle distance (kilometres) between the capitals of i and j, computed via the haversine formula. Three parameters control the shape of the kernel: λ (decay rate), p (distance exponent), and α (scaling on the focal score). Setting λ = 0 recovers the unweighted world average; setting λ very large recovers the nearest-neighbour pairwise score; intermediate values produce a regionally-weighted benchmark.

The relative-rating chart above shows Rit(d) over time: a country whose neighbours are deteriorating faster than itself will see its distance-graded relative rating climb even when its absolute score is flat—the same latent drift phenomenon documented for the M1 method in §4 of the Methodology, but now spatially weighted.

3 Caveats

Capital coordinates approximate the geographic centre of a sovereign and ignore second cities, dispersed populations, or extra-territorial dependencies. The kernel is rotation-invariant and does not distinguish overland from maritime distance—Singapore and Jakarta are 1,150 km apart, but no road connects them. For most analytical purposes the distortion is small at the regional scale; for federations and archipelagos (United States, Indonesia, Russia) a population-weighted centroid would be a closer proxy and is left for future work. The kernel parameters (λ, p, α) are not estimated—they are user-chosen, exploratory. A formal fit would require a target variable (such as bond spreads or capital-flow elasticity) against which to optimise the kernel; that exercise is documented in De, Mohapatra, and Ratha (2020) but is not reproduced here.
