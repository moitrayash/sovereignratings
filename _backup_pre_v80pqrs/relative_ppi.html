§ Relative Planetary Pressure Index

The Planetary Pressure Index, introduced by the United Nations Development Programme in the 2020 Human Development Report alongside the Planetary pressures-Adjusted Human Development Index (PHDI), captures the per-capita environmental burden a country imposes on the biosphere through its production and consumption.1 It is constructed from two normalised inputs — production-side CO2 emissions per capita and material footprint per capita — and reported as an index P ∈ [0, 1] where higher values mean a heavier per-person environmental burden. By definition PHDI = HDI × (1 − P); we recover P directly from the published PHDI/HDI ratio.

This page applies the same relative-rating logic as §4 of the Methodology to PPI, scaled by 100 (PPI100), and asks the natural question: how does a country's standing in the planetary-pressure distribution compare to its standing in the credit-rating, HDI, and Gini distributions? Note on direction: unlike HDI or credit ratings, higher PPI means worse environmental position. M2 rank and M4 percentile follow the same arithmetic, but a country at the 95th percentile of PPI is the most polluting, not the cleanest.

1 Construction

For each country-year we compute PPI100 = (1 − PHDI/HDI) × 100, then for the active peer set the leave-one-out mean and the within-peer rank statistics:

The peer set defaults to all 88 countries reporting valid PHDI for every year of the panel. The continent and preset peer-group dropdowns let it be re-cast on the fly; M1, M2, and M4 are recomputed client-side against whatever set is active.

Worked example: India PPI100 in one country-year

Step 1: recover PPI from the PHDI/HDI ratio. If India's HDI is 0.644 and PHDI is 0.633, then P = 1 − PHDI / HDI = 1 − 0.633 / 0.644 = 1 − 0.983 = 0.017. So PPI100 = 1.7.

Step 2: compute M1. If the world leave-one-out mean for PPI100 is 9.4, M1 = PPI1002 / LOO = 1.72 / 9.4 = 2.89 / 9.4 = 0.31. India's M1 sits well below the peer mean, reflecting low per-capita planetary pressure.

Step 3: compute M2 and M4. If India ranks 14th out of 88 reporting countries that year, M2 = 14 and M4 = 14 / 88 = 0.159. A low percentile here means low planetary pressure, not poor performance.

4 Caveats

The PPI is updated annually but is methodologically backward-looking: the latest year typically reflects material-footprint and CO2 data released with a one-to-three year lag. The PPI panel here ends in 2022 because UNDP HDR 2023/24 had not yet released subsequent vintages at the time of this build; updates would extend the series without changing the methodology. The world used for the leave-one-out mean is the union of countries with valid PHDI in every year of the panel (88 countries), which is narrower than the 142 covered by HDI alone — high-PPI but data-thin economies (several Gulf states, several small island states) drop out.

The CO2 input is production-based: emissions are attributed to the country where they are physically emitted rather than to the country whose consumption drove them. This makes export-oriented industrial economies (China, several East-Asian countries) look worse on PPI than a consumption-based attribution would, and consumer economies (the United States, several European countries) look better. A consumption-based PPI variant using Global Carbon Project data is on the project backlog; the difference is non-trivial.

The full PPI panel (country, year, HDI, PHDI, MF_pc, CO2_pc, PPI100) plus the relative-rating panel are in ppi_panel.csv and ppi_relative.csv respectively. The UNDP source CSV is in ppi_raw_data.zip.
