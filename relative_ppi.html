§ Relative Planetary Pressure Index

The Planetary Pressure Index, introduced by the United Nations Development Programme in the 2020 Human Development Report alongside the Planetary pressures-Adjusted Human Development Index (PHDI), captures the per-capita environmental burden a country imposes on the biosphere through its production and consumption.1 It is constructed from two normalised inputs — production-side CO2 emissions per capita and material footprint per capita — and reported as an index P ∈ [0, 1] where higher values mean a heavier per-person environmental burden. By definition PHDI = HDI × (1 − P); we recover P directly from the published PHDI/HDI ratio. This page applies the same relative-rating logic as §4 of the Methodology to PPI, scaled by 100 (PPI100), and asks the natural question: how does a country's standing in the planetary-pressure distribution compare to its standing in the credit-rating, HDI, and Gini distributions? Note on direction: unlike HDI or credit ratings, higher PPI means worse environmental position. M2 rank and M4 percentile follow the same arithmetic, but a country at the 95th percentile of PPI is the most polluting, not the cleanest.

1 Construction

The 2023/24 UNDP HDR composite-indices file2 provides annual HDI and PHDI values from 1990 through 2022 for the 88 entities for which both inputs (Material Footprint and production CO2) are reported in every year. The Planetary Pressure Index is recovered as Pit = 1 − PHDIit / HDIit. Multiplying by 100 yields PPI100 ∈ [0, 100]. For each country–year, the leave-one-out world mean is computed, and four relative measures follow the same definitions as the credit-rating M1–M4:

M1 (PPI)PPI100it2 / LOOit
M2 (PPI)rank of PPI100it within world, year t (low rank = light footprint)
M4 (PPI)M2 / N

The two inputs (CO2 per capita and material footprint per capita) are themselves rescaled by UNDP onto a common 0–1 axis using fixed planetary-boundary references — not data-driven percentiles — so the PPI is anchored to absolute environmental thresholds rather than to whichever sample of countries happens to be reporting. As a result, the world mean drifts upward over time (planetary pressure has risen, in absolute terms, almost everywhere since 1990); a country whose PPI100 is flat is therefore improving relative to its peers even though its own footprint has not fallen. The relative measures M1, M2, M4 surface this dynamic; the absolute series alone obscures it.

2 Country explorer

The Compare against control above the charts lets the comparison set be re-cast on the fly. The default is the world (all 88 reporting countries with valid PHDI). Same continent restricts the leave-one-out mean and the rank-based M2 / M4 to the focal country's UN-style continent (Africa, Asia, Europe, North America, Oceania, or South America); this makes the most sense when a country's environmental trajectory is shaped primarily by regional industrial structure (oil-exporting Gulf states against each other, low-income Sub-Saharan economies against Sub-Saharan peers). Custom subset lets you build an ad-hoc comparison set — useful for SIDS clusters, BRICS, OECD, ASEAN, G7, or any other peer grouping you care about. M1, M2, and M4 are recomputed client-side against whatever set is active.

The first chart shows absolute PPI×100 over time alongside the leave-one-out peer-set mean. The second plots the M1, M2 (rank), and M4 (percentile) trajectories. The third overlays PPI×100 on the credit composite score for the same country, both on the 0–100 scale (credit scaled up by 5/3) so the two are visually comparable. Most countries' PPI evolves slowly and monotonically (the energy mix and material economy don't restructure overnight); the credit score is more reactive and can fall sharply on a default. Where the two diverge, the underlying story is usually political — an oil exporter whose credit rating reflects fiscal strength while its environmental footprint scales with extraction; a low-income country whose creditworthiness is fragile while its per-capita pressure is structurally low.3

3 Distribution shape (latest year)

Three views of how PPI values are distributed across the world in the most recent year of the panel: the empirical CDF sorts every reporting country and shows the cumulative share at each value; the Lorenz-style curve with the inequality quotient (twice the area between the 45° equality line and the curve, computed on PPI100 values rather than incomes — here the “mass” being shared is environmental burden, not income); and the histogram with a fitted normal density overlay. The PPI distribution is much more skewed than HDI: a small handful of high-emitting, high-material-footprint countries account for a disproportionate share of total planetary pressure, so the Lorenz quotient on PPI typically runs 0.55–0.65 — well above HDI's ~0.10.

5 Caveats

The PPI is updated annually but is methodologically backward-looking: the latest year typically reflects material-footprint and CO2 data released with a one-to-three year lag. The PPI panel here ends in 2022 because UNDP HDR 2023/24 had not yet released subsequent vintages at the time of this build; updates would extend the series without changing the methodology. The world used for the leave-one-out mean is the union of countries with valid PHDI in every year of the panel (88 countries), which is narrower than the 142 covered by HDI alone — UNDP can compute PHDI only where Material Footprint estimates exist, which is a stricter coverage criterion than HDI's. Restricting the PPI benchmark to the credit-rated subset shifts the world mean upward (rated economies are systematically more industrialised) but leaves country-level relative ranks largely unchanged.

Two further caveats are specific to the index. First, the PPI is a per-capita measure: India and China have large total emissions but moderate per-capita pressure, while small high-income countries (Luxembourg, Iceland, Qatar) score high. Aggregate-mass and per-capita interpretations produce different rankings; both are legitimate, but they answer different questions. Second, production-side CO2 understates the footprint of countries that import emissions-intensive goods and overstates the footprint of countries that export them. A consumption-based variant would shift the cleanest league tables (Switzerland, Norway, France slip downward; China and India slip upward), but UNDP's headline number is the production-side figure and we follow that convention here for comparability with the published PHDI.
