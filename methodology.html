§ Methodology

This page documents the data construction, scoring conventions, and analytical methods used throughout the Explorer. For term definitions, see the Glossary; for full bibliographic detail of every reference cited below, see the Citations page. Citations follow the Chicago Manual of Style, 17th edition, author–date variant.

1 Raw Rating Data

Sources and Coverage

Credit ratings are collected from three agencies: Standard & Poor's Global Ratings (S&P), Moody's Investors Service, and DBRS Morningstar. The dataset covers 151 sovereign entities from 1949 to 2026, with 2,178 rating events in total. Coverage is uneven across agencies: S&P and Moody's rate the broadest set of countries; DBRS focuses on a smaller subset, skewed toward higher-income economies.

Numeric Scoring (0–60 Scale)

Each agency uses a letter-grade scale (e.g. AAA, AA+, AA, …, D). These are converted to a uniform numeric scale from 0 (worst) to 60 (best) for comparability across agencies. The conversion follows the conventions surveyed in Wikipedia's credit-rating overview and standardised in the academic literature by Uri Dadush and Dipak Dasgupta (2001) and Norbert Gaillard (2009).1 The approximate mapping at key grades:

Agency-level bias. DBRS Morningstar assigns systematically higher scores (mean ≈ 41.1) than Standard & Poor's (mean ≈ 31.2) or Moody's (mean ≈ 30.7), likely reflecting DBRS's narrower, higher-income coverage universe. Agency fixed effects or agency-specific normalisation should be used when pooling ratings across agencies in regressions.

2 Panel Construction

Annual Forward-Fill

Rating events are dated to the day but are sparse—a country may go years between rating actions. To construct a balanced annual panel, each country–agency rating is forward-filled: once a rating is assigned, it is carried forward to all subsequent years until a new rating event supersedes it. This reflects the convention that an outstanding rating remains in effect until it is explicitly changed.

Composite Score

For each country–year, the composite score is the simple arithmetic mean of all available agency scores for that country in that year. Countries rated by only one agency receive that agency's score as their composite; countries rated by all three receive the mean of three scores. The composite is not computed for country–years with no outstanding rating from any agency.

Composite Score CompositeScoreit = (1 / Nit) × Σa Scoreiat

where Nit = number of agencies rating country i in year t

Macro Covariates

GDP (current USD), population, and FDI net inflows (% of GDP) are sourced from the World Bank's World Development Indicators and merged by ISO3 country code. GDP per capita is derived as GDP / population. Log GDP per capita (base 10) is used in regressions to reduce skewness. Coverage: GDP ≈ 94 percent, FDI ≈ 92 percent, GDP per capita ≈ 94 percent of panel rows. Missing values: Guinea and Taiwan (the World Bank does not publish Taiwan's national accounts).

3 Peer Groups

Countries are assigned to 59 peer groups based on geographic, political, and economic criteria. Groups range from broad (World, 150 countries) to narrow (Gulf Cooperation Council, four countries). The complete list includes:

  • G-groups: G7, G8, G10, G20, G24, G15
  • European clubs: European Union, Eurozone, Schengen, NATO, Nordic, Baltic, Commonwealth of Independent States, and sub-regional groupings
  • Asian sub-regions: ASEAN/Southeast Asia, SAARC/South Asia, East Asia, GCC, Arab League, and others
  • Americas: Latin America, MERCOSUR, Andean Community, Caribbean Community, etc.
  • African sub-regions: North, West, East, Southern, Central Africa
  • World Bank income tiers: High Income, Upper Middle, Lower Middle, Low Income
  • World: all 150 rated countries, used as the global benchmark
MECE partitions. Only three groupings are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive (MECE) over all 151 countries: (1) the four World Bank income tiers; (2) the six broad continents; (3) the five African sub-regions within Africa. All other groupings intentionally overlap—do not include collinear group dummies in the same regression.

4 Relative Rating Methods (M1–M4)

Absolute scores measure a country's credit quality on a fixed scale. Relative ratings measure a country's standing within its peer group, which can change even when the absolute score is unchanged—for example, if the group mean deteriorates. Four methods are implemented, each capturing a different dimension of relative standing. The methods build on the relative-risk-rating framework of Kaushik Basu, Supriyo De, Dilip Ratha, and Hans Timmer (2013) and the relativization family surveyed by Supriyo De, Sanket Mohapatra, and Dilip Ratha (2020).2

Leave-One-Out (LOO) Group Mean

All relative methods use a leave-one-out (LOO) mean as the group benchmark. The LOO mean for country i in group g at year t is the simple mean of all other members' composite scores:

LOO Mean LOOigt = (1 / (Ngt − 1)) × Σj≠i, j∈g CompositeScorejt

Using the LOO mean prevents a country's own score from influencing its benchmark, which would mechanically compress relative ratings toward 1 and bias estimates of relative standing, particularly in small groups.

M1 — Current Relative Rating

M1 scales a country's score against the current-year LOO mean. The score is squared before dividing to amplify differences at the upper tail of the distribution (where most rated countries cluster):

M1 — Score² / Current LOO Mean M1igt = Scoreit2 / LOOigt

M1 > LOO mean indicates above-average standing; M1 < LOO mean indicates below-average standing. M1 captures both genuine rating changes and passive drift: a country holding a fixed absolute score will see its M1 rise as the group mean falls, and vice versa.

M2 — Within-Year Percentile Rank (Raw Count)

M2 is the ordinal rank of country i within its peer group in year t, expressed as a raw integer (e.g. 47 out of 150). A higher rank means a higher composite score relative to peers.

M2 — Within-Group Rank M2igt = rank of Scoreit among { Scorejt : j ∈ g, j ≠ i }

M2 is non-parametric and robust to the shape of the score distribution, but it is bounded by group size and does not distinguish between large and small score gaps.

M3 — Lagged Relative Rating

M3 is identical to M1 except the benchmark uses the prior year's LOO mean rather than the current year's:

M3 — Score² / Lagged LOO Mean M3igt = Scoreit2 / LOOig,t−1

M3 is most useful for studying the speed of adjustment in relative ratings. At the onset of a crisis, M3 is systematically more optimistic than M1: because last year's LOO mean has not yet reflected the group's deterioration, the denominator is higher, making the ratio smaller and the relative rating appear better than M1 suggests. Conversely, during recovery M3 lags behind M1 as the old (poor) group mean persists. M3 is not defined for the first year a country–group series begins (no lag available).

M4 — Within-Year Percentile (0–1 Scale)

M4 is the percentile rank of country i within its peer group, normalised to the unit interval:

M4 — Normalised Percentile Rank M4igt = M2igt / Ngt

M4 is more comparable across groups of different sizes than M2. A value of 0.5 means the country is at the median of its group. M4 saturates near 1 at the upper tail—a country at the absolute maximum score (60) will reach M4 ≈ 0.97 rather than exactly 1.0 because at least one other country (itself, excluded) is assumed to exist.

6 Gini Coefficient (Distributional Inequality)

For each peer group in each year, a Gini coefficient, originally formulated by Corrado Gini (1912),3 is computed over member composite scores to measure within-group inequality in credit quality. A Gini of 0 means all members have identical scores; a Gini of 1 means one member holds all the score mass.

Gini Coefficient Ggt = (2 / N2μ) × Σi i · Score(i)t  −  (N+1)/N

where scores are sorted in ascending order and μ is the group mean

The World Gini (group = "World") rose monotonically from 0.232 in 2000 to 0.321 in 2023, indicating that global credit inequality has grown approximately 38 percent over the past 25 years—driven by persistent divergence between high-income stable sovereigns and volatile emerging/frontier markets.

7 Panel Regression Framework

The main regression dataset (paper_panel.csv) supports the following specifications. All panel regressions use clustered standard errors at the country level, following the convention discussed in A. Colin Cameron and Pravin K. Trivedi's Microeconometrics: Methods and Applications.4

ModelSpecificationFixed EffectsKey Finding
A Score ~ GDP growth + FDI Country + Year R²(within) ≈ 0 — macro flows explain almost no within-country variation
B Score ~ GDP growth + FDI + log GDPpc Country + Year log GDPpc β ≈ 23.3*** within FE (collinear with country FE — inflated)
C Score ~ GDP growth + FDI + log GDP(bn) Country + Year Similar to B; GDP level vs per capita comparison
D (Pooled OLS) Score ~ GDP growth + FDI + log GDPpc None R² = 0.90; log GDPpc β ≈ 9.2*** — cross-sectional variation dominates

The stark contrast between Models A and D (R²(within) ≈ 0 vs R²(overall) = 0.90) is the central empirical result: credit scores are almost entirely determined by structural country characteristics rather than annual macroeconomic fluctuations. The between-country component—richer countries get better ratings—dwarfs any within-country year-to-year variation.

8 Ordered Model for Rating Transitions

A separate ordered logit and ordered probit, following Daniel McFadden (1974),5 are estimated on the sample of annual rating transitions (N = 2,922 country–year pairs), where the dependent variable takes three values:

  • −1: downgrade (composite score fell from prior year) — 13 percent of observations
  • 0: stable (no change) — 70 percent of observations
  • +1: upgrade (composite score rose from prior year) — 17 percent of observations

Regressors: GDP growth (prior year), log GDP per capita, FDI (% GDP), and lagged composite score. Key results: GDP growth and log GDPpc are positively associated with upgrades; the lagged score is negatively associated (mean reversion—already high-rated countries have less room to rise); FDI is insignificant.

The qualitative narrative of the paper is invariant to scale choice.

10 Basu Relative Risk Rating & CRIS

Kaushik Basu, Supriyo De, Dilip Ratha, and Hans Timmer (2013) introduce a Relative Risk Rating (RR) for sovereigns, designed to strip out the global-cycle component of agency ratings and isolate each country's standing relative to a weighted world benchmark.6 The same paper proposes CRIS (Country-level Risk-Indexing System), a closely related ratio index expressed as a percentage of the world average.

Relative Risk Rating (RR)

Basu RR RRit = rit − Σj wjt · rjt

where wjt are GDP weights and rjt is country j's score in year t

Four versions are computed (saved in basu_cris_panel.csv): GDP-weighted with time-varying weights, GDP-weighted with weights fixed at 2008 (Basu et al.'s preferred convention, neutralising weight drift), and the same two on the directional score_60 scale. The 2008-weight version isolates pure rating change from changes in the relative economic mass of each country.

CRIS (Ratio Index)

CRIS — Country Risk Index CRISit = 100 × rit / (Σj wjt · rjt)

CRIS expresses each country's score as a percentage of the GDP-weighted world average. CRIS = 100 means a country sits exactly at the world benchmark; CRIS > 100 means it is rated above the global average, < 100 below.

Equivalence with M1–M4. The Basu et al. RR / CRIS family and the De-style relativizations (next section) correlate at r ≥ 0.93 with this project's M1–M4 relative rating measures. They are the same idea—re-expressing a country's score relative to a peer benchmark—implemented with different weighting (equal vs GDP vs population) and centring (subtraction vs division vs z-score) choices.

Where the Explorer's M1 squares the score before dividing, the De variants stay linear, making them easier to compare across scales but less sensitive to upper-tail discrimination.

12 Shadow Rating Model

Following Dilip Ratha, Supriyo De, and Sanket Mohapatra (2007/2011), a shadow rating is the rating implied by a country's macro and institutional fundamentals, estimated by regressing observed composite scores on a set of structural covariates.8 The shadow rating is then applied to all 150 countries—including those rated by no agency or only one—and the residual (Actual − Shadow) measures how far each agency's call sits above or below what fundamentals predict.

Residuals by region (S2 benchmark)

The average gap between actual ratings and the shadow rating, conditional on a country being rated, is small for Africa (≈ −0.8 notches), modestly negative for South America (−2.9 to −3.6 across agencies), and consistently positive for North America and Oceania (+6 to +7 for DBRS). The most under-rated countries are in active distress (Sri Lanka, Ghana, Ukraine, Ethiopia, Barbados); the most over-rated are advanced economies (European Union, United States, Germany, Canada, Australia, +11 to +17), suggesting an “advanced-economy premium” not explained by macro and institutional fundamentals. The detailed political economy of these residuals is parsed in the Stories page; the African under-rating critique is taken up most directly in the UNDP report on lowering the cost of borrowing in Africa.10

13 Pairwise & Distance-Graded Ratings

Two finer-grained relativizations extend the M1–M4 family: a pairwise measure that compares one country directly against another (PABt = ScoreAt − ScoreBt), and a distance-graded measure that weights every other country by an inverse-distance kernel from the focal capital (the haversine great-circle distance with an exponential-decay kernel). Both are implemented in the interactive Pairwise & Distance-Graded Ratings page, with sliders for the kernel parameters λ (decay), p (exponent), and α (focal scaling).
