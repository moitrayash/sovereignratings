1 Raw Rating Data

Sources and Coverage

Credit ratings are collected from three agencies: Standard & Poor's Global Ratings (S&P), Moody's Investors Service, and DBRS. The dataset covers 151 sovereign entities from 1949 to 2026, with 2,178 rating events in total. Coverage is uneven across agencies: S&P and Moody's rate the broadest set of countries; DBRS focuses on a smaller subset, skewed toward higher-income economies.

Numeric Scoring (0–60 Scale)

Each agency uses a letter-grade scale (e.g. AAA, AA+, AA, …, D). These are converted to a uniform numeric scale from 0 (worst) to 60 (best) for comparability across agencies. The conversion is the “refined Basu mapping”: each major letter grade is associated with a 6-point band, each notch within the grade is 3 points, and outlooks (positive, stable, negative) shift the within-notch score by ±1 point.1

Worked example 1.1: Converting an S&P rating to a 0–60 score

Suppose S&P assigns India the rating BBB− with a stable outlook on 18 May 2024. Step through the conversion:

1. Locate BBB− in the major-grade table → BBB band starts at 42, BBB− is the lowest of the three notches (BBB+, BBB, BBB−), so its base score is 42 − 3 = 39. 2. Apply outlook adjustment: stable → 0; positive → +1; negative → −1. Stable here, so add 0. 3. Final score = 39.

Then S&P upgrades India to BBB stable on 14 August 2024:

1. BBB (the middle notch in the band) base score = 42. 2. Stable outlook → 0. 3. Final score = 42.

The rating event has moved the score by +3 points = one full notch.

Worked example 1.2: Converting a Moody's rating with negative outlook

Moody's rates Pakistan Caa3 with a negative outlook in early 2023.

1. Caa is a single 6-point band centred at score 12; Caa3 is the lowest of three notches (Caa1, Caa2, Caa3), so its base score is 12 − 3 = 9. 2. Negative outlook → −1. 3. Final score = 8.

This places Pakistan two notches above the SD/D floor and clearly in the distress zone.

Agency-level bias. DBRS assigns systematically higher scores (mean ≈ 41.1) than Standard & Poor's (mean ≈ 31.2) or Moody's (mean ≈ 30.7), likely reflecting DBRS's narrower, higher-income coverage universe. Agency fixed effects or agency-specific normalisation should be used when pooling ratings across agencies in regressions.

Why a 0–60 scale rather than 0–20 or 1–21?

The 0–60 convention multiplies the standard 21-notch academic scale (e.g. used by Reinhart and Rogoff 2009 and by ECB working papers) by approximately three. It then assigns each notch a 3-point block and reserves the within-block units for the outlook adjustment. Three competing scales were considered seriously:

Worked example 1.3: Converting one country between all eight scales

Take a hypothetical country rated A− (S&P) with stable outlook. The mapping across the eight scales is:

score_60: A is at 48, A− subtracts 3 → 45. Stable outlook adds 0. score_21: A is rank 16, A− rank 15. score_20: A is 14.3, A− is 13.6 (linear rescale of score_21). score_17: A− falls into rank 12 in the Afonso, Furceri, and Gomes mapping (which collapses the bottom four notches). score_16: rank 11 in the Cantor and Packer 16-notch mapping. score_basu60: 60 − 45 = 15 (smaller is better in the Basu mirror). score_norm01: 45 / 60 = 0.75. score_logPD: A− corresponds to a one-year Moody's idealised PD of about 0.040%; −ln(0.0004) ≈ 7.82.

All eight numbers describe the same underlying letter rating. Spearman correlations across the eight columns are exactly 1 by construction (they are monotonic transforms of each other within agency, save for the directional flip in the Basu mirror).

Why this scale and not a slightly different one? Three reasons:

  1. Outlook integration without fractions. A 21-notch scale forces the outlook adjustment into fractional units (BBB stable = 13, BBB positive = 13.33). The 60-point scale uses integers throughout because each notch is 3 points wide and the outlook shift is ±1 inside the notch.
  2. Directional intuition. Higher = better matches the convention used by every agency's marketing materials and by most pricing models, even though the Basu et al. (2013) original CRIS paper uses the inverted convention (smaller = better) so that the index reads as a quasi-default-probability. The two are monotonic mirror images and contain identical information.
  3. Distress-zone precision. The 17-notch and 16-notch scales collapse the bottom notches into one or two bins, which is fine for cross-sectional analysis of investment-grade sovereigns but loses information in exactly the part of the distribution where our most interesting stories live (Argentina 2001, Greece 2012, Sri Lanka 2022). The 60-point scale preserves CCC+, CCC, CCC−, CC, C, SD, and D as distinct values.

Crucially, the choice of scale does not change any qualitative conclusion in this project. Section 9 shows the Models A–D regressions re-run on every scale variant; coefficients change in magnitude proportionally with the y-variable's range, but signs and significance levels are identical. The discussion is in Amartya Sen's 1977 paper on weights and measures: a monotonic cardinal transformation of an ordinal index preserves all rank-order analysis.2

2 Panel Construction

Annual Forward-Fill

Rating events are dated to the day but are sparse—a country may go years between rating actions. To construct a balanced annual panel, each country–agency rating is forward-filled: once a rating is assigned, it is carried forward to all subsequent years until a new rating event supersedes it. This reflects the convention that an outstanding rating remains in effect until it is explicitly changed.

Composite Score

For each country–year, the composite score is the simple arithmetic mean of all available agency scores for that country in that year. Countries rated by only one agency receive that agency's score as their composite; countries rated by all three receive the mean of three scores.

Composite Score CompositeScoreit = (1 / Nit) × Σa Scoreiat

where Nit = number of agencies rating country i in year t
Worked example 2.1: Computing the composite for a fully-rated country

Brazil in 2020 was rated by all three agencies: S&P at BB− (score 33), Moody's at Ba2 (score 36), DBRS at BB (high) (score 38).

Composite = (33 + 36 + 38) / 3 = 35.67

This sits one and a half notches below the lowest investment-grade boundary (42) and reflects the typical “DBRS rates high” bias visible across the panel.

Worked example 2.2: Composite when only one agency rates the country

Belize in 2018 was rated only by S&P, which had it at CC (score 12). Moody's withdrew in 2008; DBRS does not cover Belize.

Composite = 12 / 1 = 12

Care is needed comparing single-agency composites with three-agency composites: the former carry the systematic bias of whichever agency happened to cover the country. For sub-Saharan and Caribbean countries this is often an issue; the shadow rating approach in §12 partially corrects for it by predicting an “agency-neutral” rating from fundamentals.

Macro Covariates

GDP (current USD), population, and FDI net inflows (% of GDP) are sourced from the World Development Indicators and merged by ISO3 country code. GDP per capita is derived as GDP / population. Log GDP per capita (base 10) is used in regressions to reduce skewness. Coverage: GDP ≈ 94 percent, FDI ≈ 92 percent, GDP per capita ≈ 94 percent of panel rows. Missing values: Guinea and Taiwan (the World Bank does not publish Taiwan's national accounts).

3 Peer Groups

Countries are assigned to 59 peer groups based on geographic, political and economic criteria. Groups range from broad (World, 150 countries) to narrow (Gulf Cooperation Council, four countries). The complete list includes G-groups, European clubs, Asian sub-regions, Americas blocs, African sub-regions, World Bank income tiers, and the World group.

MECE partitions. Only three groupings are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive (MECE) over all 151 countries: (1) the four World Bank income tiers; (2) the six broad continents; (3) the five African sub-regions within Africa. All other groupings intentionally overlap—do not include collinear group dummies in the same regression.

4 Relative Rating Methods (M1–M4)

Absolute scores measure a country's credit quality on a fixed scale. Relative ratings measure a country's standing within its peer group. Four methods are implemented, building on the relative-risk-rating framework introduced by Dr. Kaushik Basu, Supriyo De, Dilip Ratha, and Hans Timmer (2013), and the broader relativization family surveyed by De, Sanket Mohapatra and Ratha (2020).3

Leave-One-Out (LOO) Group Mean

All relative methods use a leave-one-out (LOO) mean as the group benchmark.

LOO Mean LOOigt = (1 / (Ngt − 1)) × Σj≠i, j∈g CompositeScorejt
Worked example 4.1: LOO for the GCC peer group

The Gulf Cooperation Council in 2024 has six members with the following composite scores: Bahrain 30, Kuwait 50, Oman 38, Qatar 51, Saudi Arabia 48, United Arab Emirates 53.

Saudi Arabia's LOO mean (excluding itself):

LOO = (30 + 50 + 38 + 51 + 53) / 5 = 222 / 5 = 44.40

Saudi Arabia's M1: 48² / 44.40 = 51.89. Above the LOO mean by 7.5 points, so M1 > LOO indicating above-average GCC standing.

Worked example 4.2: LOO for the World group (large N)

India 2024: composite = 33. World group N = 150 reporting countries with sum-of-scores 4,977.

LOO = (4977 − 33) / 149 = 4944 / 149 = 33.18

For a 150-member group, removing one country changes the mean only by 1/149 of the country's deviation from the simple mean—negligible. For the GCC (N=6) above, removing Saudi Arabia changes the mean by a much larger fraction, which is exactly why the LOO correction is necessary in small groups.

M1: Current Relative Rating

M1: Score² / Current LOO Mean M1igt = Scoreit² / LOOigt
Worked example 4.3: M1 for India in the BRICS group, 2020

BRICS in 2020 (Brazil 36, Russia 36, India 33, China 51, South Africa 30). India's LOO:

LOO = (36 + 36 + 51 + 30) / 4 = 38.25 M1 = 33² / 38.25 = 1089 / 38.25 = 28.47

M1 = 28.47 vs LOO = 38.25: India is below its BRICS peer average by ~10 score points, and its M1 sits accordingly below the LOO benchmark.

M2: Within-Year Percentile Rank (Raw Count)

M2: Within-Group Rank M2igt = rank of Scoreit among { Scorejt : j ∈ g }
Worked example 4.4: M2 for India in the BRICS group, 2020

Sorted BRICS scores 2020: South Africa 30 (rank 1), India 33 (rank 2), Brazil 36 (rank 3 tied), Russia 36 (rank 3 tied), China 51 (rank 5).

M2(India) = 2 out of 5

M3: Lagged Relative Rating

M3: Score² / Lagged LOO Mean M3igt = Scoreit² / LOOig,t−1

M3 is most useful for studying the speed of adjustment in relative ratings. At crisis onset, M3 is systematically more optimistic than M1 because last year's LOO mean has not yet absorbed the group's deterioration.

M4: Within-Year Percentile (0–1)

6 Gini Coefficient (Distributional Inequality)

For each peer group in each year, a Gini coefficient, originally formulated by Corrado Gini (1912),4 is computed over member composite scores to measure within-group inequality in credit quality. A Gini of 0 means all members have identical scores; a Gini of 1 means one member holds all the score mass.

Gini Coefficient Ggt = (2 / N²μ) × Σi i · Score(i)t  −  (N+1)/N

where scores are sorted in ascending order and μ is the group mean
Worked example 6.1: Gini for the GCC in 2024

Sorted: Bahrain 30, Oman 38, Saudi Arabia 48, Kuwait 50, Qatar 51, UAE 53. N = 6, μ = 45.

Σ i·Score = 1·30 + 2·38 + 3·48 + 4·50 + 5·51 + 6·53 = 30+76+144+200+255+318 = 1023 G = 2·1023 / (6²·45) − (6+1)/6 = 2046/1620 − 1.167 = 1.263 − 1.167 = 0.097

A Gini below 0.1 indicates very tight clustering: the GCC is a homogeneous high-rated group.

The World Gini rose monotonically from 0.232 in 2000 to 0.321 in 2023, indicating that global credit inequality has grown approximately 38 percent over the past 25 years.

8 Ordered Model for Rating Transitions

A separate ordered logit and ordered probit, following Daniel McFadden (1974),6 are estimated on the sample of annual rating transitions (N = 2,922 country–year pairs).

9 Alternative Numerical Scales: Invariance Check

The 0–60 composite is the primary scale, but it is one of several conventions in the academic and policy literature. To verify that paper conclusions do not depend on the conversion convention, the panel is re-expressed on the eight scales in §1. Pairwise correlations among the eight scales are r ≥ 0.99 for all monotone transforms (the Basu mirror is r = −1 with score_60 by construction). Re-running Models A–D under each scale leaves every coefficient identical in sign and statistical significance; magnitudes scale exactly with the y-variable's range. The qualitative narrative of the paper is invariant to scale choice.

10 Relative Risk Rating & the Comparative Rating Index for Sovereigns (CRIS)

Dr. Kaushik Basu, Supriyo De, Dilip Ratha, and Hans Timmer (2013) introduce the Relative Risk Rating (RR) for sovereigns and the closely related Comparative Rating Index for Sovereigns (CRIS).7

Relative Risk Rating (RR)

Relative Risk Rating RRit = rit − Σj wjt · rjt
Worked example 10.1: RR for India 2020 with three peers, GDP-weighted

Suppose the world consists of India (score 33, GDP $2.7T), United States (60, $21T), China (51, $14T), Japan (60, $5T). Total GDP = $42.7T.

GDP-weighted world score = (33·2.7 + 60·21 + 51·14 + 60·5) / 42.7 = (89.1 + 1260 + 714 + 300) / 42.7 = 2363.1 / 42.7 = 55.34 RR(India) = 33 − 55.34 = −22.34

India's RR is strongly negative: it sits ~22 score points below the GDP-weighted world average. The choice of weighting matters enormously here—simple equal weighting would give a world average of 51, RR = −18, a very different number. Basu et al. argue for GDP weighting because the relevant counterfactual for global capital allocation is the wealth-weighted average risk in the system.

Comparative Rating Index for Sovereigns (CRIS)

CRIS CRISit = 100 × rit / (Σj wjt · rjt)
Worked example 10.2: CRIS for the same India 2020 case
CRIS(India) = 100 × 33 / 55.34 = 59.6

India sits at 59.6 percent of the GDP-weighted world rating. CRIS = 100 means a country is exactly at the world benchmark; CRIS > 100 above-average, < 100 below.

Four versions of RR/CRIS are computed (saved in basu_cris_panel.csv): GDP-weighted with time-varying weights, GDP-weighted with weights fixed at 2008 (Basu et al.'s preferred convention, neutralising weight drift), and the same two on the directional score_60 scale.

Equivalence with M1–M4. RR / CRIS and the linear relativizations (next section) correlate at r ≥ 0.93 with this project's M1–M4. They are the same idea—re-expressing a country's score relative to a peer benchmark—implemented with different weighting (equal vs GDP vs population) and centring (subtraction vs division vs z-score) choices.

Worked example 11.1: Comparing all 10 variants for one country

India 2020: Score = 33. World stats (rated countries): equal-weighted mean = 35.5, GDP-weighted mean = 55.3, population-weighted mean = 31.8, median = 33, SD = 16.2, max = 60, min = 0.

rel_eq = 33 − 35.5 = −2.5 rel_gdp = 33 − 55.3 = −22.3 rel_pop = 33 − 31.8 = +1.2 rel_median = 33 − 33 = 0 z_eq = (33 − 35.5) / 16.2 = −0.154 z_gdp = (33 − 55.3) / 16.2 = −1.378 pct_rank ≈ 0.45 (India ranks ~67/150 from the bottom) norm_max = 33 / 60 = 0.55 norm_min = (33 − 0) / (60 − 0) = 0.55

The variants disagree dramatically about whether India is “above” or “below” average, depending on the weighting choice. Population-weighted? India is above average. GDP-weighted? Far below. The Basu CRIS framework lets you tag which weighting you find substantively most relevant; M1–M4 sidestep the question by using equal weighting within named peer groups.

12 Shadow Rating Model

Following Dilip Ratha, Supriyo De and Sanket Mohapatra (2007/2011), a shadow rating is the rating implied by a country's macro and institutional fundamentals.9 Five nested OLS specifications are estimated (S0–S4); the preferred benchmark is S2 (R² = 0.76, RMSE = 8.05 points ≈ 2.7 letter-grade notches). Macro covariates from World Development Indicators; Rule of Law from the Worldwide Governance Indicators, methodology in Daniel Kaufmann, Aart Kraay and Massimo Mastruzzi (2010).10

Worked example 12.1: Computing the shadow rating for one country (Spec S2)

S2 fitted coefficients (pooled OLS, cluster-robust SE):

const = −10.23 · log GDPpc = +11.25*** · GDP_growth = +0.026 · GrowthVol_5y = −0.074* · Inflation = −0.105*** · RuleOfLaw = +7.53*** · CurrentAccount = +0.241***

India 2020 covariates: log GDPpc = 3.30, GDP_growth = −5.83, GrowthVol_5y = 3.1, Inflation = 6.2, RuleOfLaw = −0.05, CurrentAccount = 0.86.

Shadow = −10.23 + 11.25·3.30 + 0.026·(−5.83) + (−0.074)·3.1 + (−0.105)·6.2 + 7.53·(−0.05) + 0.241·0.86 = −10.23 + 37.13 − 0.15 − 0.23 − 0.65 − 0.38 + 0.21 ≈ 25.7

India's actual composite in 2020 was 35. Residual = Actual − Shadow = +9.3, meaning the agencies rated India almost three notches above what fundamentals predict. (Unusual; in 2023 the residual narrowed considerably as the Indian economy outperformed the model's growth assumption.)

13 Paired & Distance-Graded Ratings

Two finer-grained relativizations extend the M1–M4 family: a paired measure that compares one country directly against another (PABt = ScoreAt − ScoreBt), and a distance-graded measure that weights every other country by an inverse-distance kernel from the focal capital (the haversine great-circle distance with an exponential-decay kernel). Both are implemented on the interactive Paired, Grouped & Regional Ratings and Distance-graded Ratings pages.
