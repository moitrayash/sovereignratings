§ Distance-graded Ratings

The paired measure compares two countries; a flat group average treats all peers equally. A distance-graded measure interpolates between these two extremes by weighting every other country by an inverse-distance kernel from the focal capital,1 using the haversine great-circle distance between coordinates.

Distance-weighted benchmark Bit(d) = (Σj≠i wij Scorejt) / (Σj≠i wij)

Kernel wij = exp(−λ · (dij / 1000)p)

Distance-graded relative score Rit(d) = α · Scoreit − Bit(d)

Three parameters control the kernel: λ (decay rate), p (distance exponent) and α (scaling on the focal country). Setting λ = 0 recovers the unweighted world average; setting λ very large recovers the nearest-neighbour paired score; intermediate values produce a regionally-weighted benchmark. The exponential-decay specification is the standard kernel discussed in spatial econometrics2 and is closely related to the geographically-weighted regression literature.

2 Caveats

Capital coordinates approximate the geographic centre of a sovereign and ignore second cities, dispersed populations, or extra-territorial dependencies. The kernel does not distinguish overland from maritime distance. For most analytical purposes the distortion is small at the regional scale; for federations and archipelagos a population-weighted centroid would be a closer proxy and is left for future work. The kernel parameters (λ, p, α) are not estimated—they are user-chosen, exploratory. A formal fit would require a target variable (such as bond spreads or capital-flow elasticity) against which to optimise the kernel; that exercise is documented in De, Mohapatra and Ratha (2020) but is not reproduced here.
