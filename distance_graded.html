§ Distance-graded Ratings

The paired measure compares two countries; a flat group average treats all peers equally. A distance-graded measure interpolates between these two extremes by weighting every other country by an inverse-distance kernel from the focal capital,1 using the haversine great-circle distance between coordinates.

1 The math, plainly

Distance-weighted benchmark Bit(d) = (Σj≠i wij Scorejt) / (Σj≠i wij)

Kernel wij = exp(−λ · (dij / 1000)p)

Distance-graded relative score Rit(d) = α · Scoreit − Bit(d)

Haversine distance dij = 2R · arcsin(√(sin²(Δφ/2) + cos(φi)·cos(φj)·sin²(Δλ/2))) where R = 6371 km, φ = latitude, λ = longitude

Intuition for each parameter

The kernel wij assigns a weight between 0 and 1 to each peer country j as seen from the focal country i. Three knobs control how that weight schedule looks:

λ (decay rate) — how fast weight falls off with distance. Setting λ = 0 recovers the unweighted world average (every country gets weight 1, so the benchmark is just the global mean). Setting λ very large recovers the nearest-neighbour paired score (only the closest country gets meaningful weight). Intermediate values produce a regionally-weighted benchmark. The half-distance — the radius at which weight has decayed to 0.5 — is d½ = 1000·(ln 2 / λ)1/p km. With λ = 0.6 and p = 1, the half-distance is about 1155 km; with λ = 1.5 it is about 462 km.

p (distance exponent) — the curvature of the falloff. With p = 1, the kernel is a pure exponential — gradual, smooth decay. With p < 1 (sub-linear) the falloff is gentler far away but sharper near zero, spreading weight further. With p > 1 (super-linear) the falloff is gentler near zero but very sharp far away, concentrating weight more tightly around the focal country. The choice of p alters the shape of the influence cone, not its size.

α (scaling on the focal country) — the multiplier on the focal country's own score before subtracting the benchmark. With α = 1, R(d) is a centred difference that reads like a paired spread (positive = focal country is higher than its kernel benchmark; negative = lower). With α > 1, the absolute level of the focal score is amplified before subtraction — useful when you want to ask “is this country's score above what its level plus peer-context predicts?”.

Consequences of changing each parameter

To make this concrete, fix the focal country (say, India), the year (2024), and toggle one parameter at a time. The full kernel response surface looks like this:

  1. Move λ from 0 → ∞ at p=1, α=1. R(d) moves smoothly from (focal − global mean) to (focal − nearest neighbour). For India in 2024 that journey is from R = 35 − 32 = +3 (mild outperformance vs world) to R = 35 − 13.5 = +21.5 (large outperformance vs Pakistan, the geographic nearest neighbour with rating data). Identical absolute score; ten-times-different relative reading. The choice of λ encodes the analyst's prior about “how much should I let geographic proximity dictate the comparison?”
  2. Move p from 0.4 → 2.5 at fixed λ. At p=0.4 the kernel is heavy-tailed: countries 5,000+ km away still carry meaningful weight, so the benchmark is closer to a global mean. At p=2.5 the kernel is sharply peaked: weight collapses to near-neighbours and the benchmark is effectively a paired comparison with the nearest 2–3 capitals. p is a “regional-vs-global” dial.
  3. Move α from 0.5 → 2 at fixed kernel. The benchmark is unchanged but the focal score is rescaled before subtraction. At α=0.5, R(d) reads like “half the focal level, less the regional benchmark” — exaggerating relative weakness. At α=2, R(d) doubles the focal level — exaggerating relative strength. Most analytical use cases want α=1; α≠1 is useful when stress-testing the sensitivity of the relative reading to the level of the focal series itself.

The kernel is a continuous spatial filter: there is no hard cutoff. India's distance-graded benchmark always includes Iceland with some non-zero weight; that weight is just very small — at λ=0.6, p=1, Iceland's weight is roughly e−0.6 × 6.4 ≈ 0.022, compared to Pakistan's ≈ 0.69 and Bangladesh's ≈ 0.78. The benchmark is a continuous gradient from neighbours to far-flung peers, not a binary in/out decision.

2 Caveats

Capital coordinates approximate the geographic centre of a sovereign and ignore second cities, dispersed populations, or extra-territorial dependencies. The kernel does not distinguish overland from maritime distance. For most analytical purposes the distortion is small at the regional scale; for federations and archipelagos a population-weighted centroid would be a closer proxy and is left for future work. The kernel parameters (λ, p, α) are not estimated—they are user-chosen, exploratory. A formal fit would require a target variable (such as bond spreads or capital-flow elasticity) against which to optimise the kernel; that exercise is documented in De, Mohapatra and Ratha (2020) but is not reproduced here.
